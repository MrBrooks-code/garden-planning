Memorial Meadow

A field guide for planting day

Racine · Wisconsin · Zone 6a · Clay-heavy soil

One hundred twenty-five native plants across twenty-seven species, arrayed along an L-shaped meadow band that wraps from the southern gate to the northern peach trees. The garden is dedicated to recently-lost dogs. The memorial plant is Wild Columbine — chosen because it self-seeds and returns in slightly different places each year, like memory.

The Walk — A Formality Gradient

The design is composed along the path arc. Brooks enters at the gate, walks west along the south path, turns north at the elbow, and exits toward the peach trees. The arrangement follows formal-to-wild composition along this arc: clearest drifts at the gate, scattered matrix in the middle, sparse taper at the north end.

★ memorial plant (Wild Columbine)   ·   ♢ moisture-loving — concentrate at the wet pocket

Notes on the Site

Soil. Clay-heavy throughout. Holds moisture deep but the surface dries fast. Do not amend planting holes with compost — backfill with native clay. The cardboard-and-mulch sheet over the scalped lawn is the right preparation.

Water. The meadow is not as wet as “Zone 6a clay” might suggest. Early spring is wet from snowmelt; once that’s gone the site moves to moist-but-needs-watering through summer. The single exception is the south-bulge wet pocket, where the gutter (G) empties and the existing turtleheads (Y) thrive. Moisture-loving plants concentrate there — they’re marked ♢ in each segment table.

Existing plantings that anchor the design. Hazelnut (H), red osier dogwood (W), midnight express redbud (M), viburnum (V), comfrey (C), ninebark (N), toka plum (O), smooth hydrangea (S), pussywillow (X), and the two white turtleheads (Y) in the wet pocket — all stay where they are.

Planting Workflow

  1. Cut an X through the cardboard with a sharp knife. Fold the flaps back.
  2. Dig a hole into the clay below — twice as wide as the root ball, the same depth.
  3. Loosen the root ball. Score the sides if pot-bound; pull circling roots apart.
  4. Set the plant with the top of the root ball level with the surrounding soil.
  5. Backfill with native clay. Do not amend with compost.
  6. Fold the cardboard flaps back around the stem, leaving 2–3″ of clearance.
  7. Re-mulch the collar, keeping mulch 2″ away from the stem. No mulch volcanoes.
  8. Water deeply, immediately. Saturate the root ball and the surrounding clay.

Spacing Reference

Small (Geum, Allium, Heuchera, Aquilegia, Ginger)10–12″ apart
Medium (Penstemon, Liatris, Aster, Coreopsis)14–18″ apart
Large (Milkweed, Bergamot, Mountain Mint, Coneflower)18–22″ apart
Structural (Joe Pye, Culver’s Root, Ironweed, Rattlesnake Master)30–36″ apart
Grasses & sedges10–12″ apart

Plant tighter than nursery tags suggest. Clay plus sheet-mulch closes canopy faster than expected.

First-Year Care

  • Weeks 1–2Water every 2–3 days, deeply.
  • Weeks 3–8Water 1–2× per week, deeply.
  • Weeks 9 onwardWater only in drought. Hand-water the wet-pocket species and the Joe Pye in dry stretches.
  • Early JunePinch tips on New England Aster, Smooth Blue Aster, and Sneezeweed by one-third.
  • Late JulyCut Spiderwort back to 6″ after bloom.
  • FallDo not cut anything back. Leave stems for winter habitat and seed-food.
  • Year 2, Mar–AprCut back the previous year’s dead stems.
